Scout

An embedded AI pricing co-pilot. This is the complete behavioral specification — every surface, state, transition, and consideration needed to build it.

Written as design intent: it describes what the product does and how it behaves, not how the prototype was coded. Numbers (timings, sizes) are intended behavior, not implementation mandates. 32 states are captured with annotated screenshots — click any image to enlarge.

Overview

Overview

Scout is an embedded AI pricing co-pilot that lives inside a dense pricing grid — the surface where an analyst sets and publishes fuel prices per customer, location, and product. It answers questions about the rows on screen: why a row is flagged, where margin is being left on the table, how the market moved, and how a price ranks against a benchmark. Every answer explains its reasoning, cites its sources, and states a confidence level, and the analyst can save reusable questions and multi-step paths, share them with teammates, and let Scout work in the background across many rows at once.

The mental model fits in one line: ask in plain language → get an explainable, scoped answer → save it for reuse → let Scout work in the background and tell you when it's done. Everything that follows is in service of keeping that loop calm and legible.

Entry points

Scout is reachable four ways, each tuned to a different moment in the analyst's work.

  • Toolbar trigger. A control in the grid's toolbar labelled Ask Scout, carrying a small 8-bit mascot head, opens the panel. On first visit a one-time intro bubble points at it (see 2): Hi, I'm Scout 👋 — Your pricing co-pilot. Ask about any row — costs, margins, deltas. Click to start, or press ⌘G.
  • Keyboard shortcut. Pressing ⌘G / Ctrl+G opens the panel from anywhere. It is open-only — it never toggles the panel closed — and is ignored while the analyst is typing in another input.
  • Per-row ready indicator. A small three-dot marker in a pinned grid column lights up when a background answer for that row is ready; selecting it jumps straight into that row's conversation.
  • Cross-page alert. When Scout finishes thinking while the analyst is on another page, a name tag reading Scout is ready and alert cards in the app's Control Panel invite them back.

Design tone

The intended feel is calm, trustworthy, and quietly intelligent — "a thinking machine." The brand color is a deep violet (#715DAA) and the type is Lato. Motion is present but restrained: a slow shimmer while Scout thinks, gentle slides between views, never anything bouncy.

Trust is structural, not decorative. Confidence and sources accompany every answer by design. They are core to why an analyst would act on what Scout says, not visual garnish.
Information architecture

Information architecture

Scout lives in a single panel that holds two top-level views, reached through a tab strip: Chats and Library. Everything the analyst does — asking, reviewing past conversations, and reusing saved questions — happens inside this one surface, so navigation never leaves the pricing grid behind.

The tab strip is the panel's primary wayfinding control (see 1). Chats is list-first: it opens onto the full set of conversations rather than a single thread. Each conversation appears as a card; selecting one slides into that conversation, and a back affordance labeled ‹ All Chats returns to the list. There is no separate inbox view — the Chats list is the inbox, carrying an unseen-count badge when background answers are ready.

The Library view holds reusable saved items: Prompt (one question) and Path (an ordered sequence of questions). It offers search, a starred-only filter, and an ownership filter spanning All, Yours, and Shared with you.

Scoping

Scoping is the cross-cutting concept: whatever the analyst selects in the grid — a row, a single cell, or a multi-cell selection — becomes the conversation's context, and Scout answers about that scope; with nothing in focus, it answers about the whole view.

Panel modes

The panel presents in one of two modes, switched by a single control. It can float as a draggable, resizable card docked top-right, or dock as a sidebar at full height, pushing the grid leftward to make room.

SurfacePurpose
Chats viewList-first home for every conversation; opening a card slides into that thread.
Library viewSaved Prompts and Paths, with search, starred-only, and ownership filters.
Floating modeDraggable, height-resizable card docked top-right, overlaying the grid.

Panel anatomy & layout

Panel anatomy & layout

The panel is a single vertical stack of five regions, ordered top to bottom, that stays consistent whether Scout floats as a card or docks as a full-height sidebar. Everything an analyst can do to Scout itself lives in these regions; a handful of supporting surfaces deliberately render outside the panel.

Top-to-bottom chrome

  1. Title row — the mascot mark and Scout wordmark sit on the left; a control cluster sits on the right, in order: Save as path, Clear, Mode (float/dock), Minimize, and Close. Save-as-path and Clear appear greyed and inert when there is nothing to act on (see 2, 3). In floating mode this whole row doubles as the drag handle (see 1).
  2. View nav — the Chats / Library tab strip. The Chats tab carries an unseen-count badge when background answers are ready. Inside an open conversation, a ‹ All Chats back control replaces the tab strip.
  3. Context-chips bar (Chats only) — the current scope shown as removable pills, or, when empty, the hint Nothing in focus · click a cell alongside a + Add a cell affordance.
  4. Body — the active view: welcome, conversation, chats list, or library. It slides horizontally when switching views, the push/pop direction matching the nav direction.
  5. Composer footer (Chats only) — a multi-line input that grows with content, placeholder Ask Scout about your quote book…, a Send button (inert while empty or busy), and a circular Undo affordance to the left that reverses the last turn.

Geometry & behavior

In floating mode the panel anchors top-right with a margin and auto-fits to its content height, growing until it reaches a cap of roughly 80vh, after which the body scrolls and the chrome stays put. A resize grip at the bottom-right adjusts height only — width is fixed in floating mode and the cursor reads as a vertical-resize. Dragging the title row repositions the floating card; that position persists across a minimize and resets only on discard.

The Mode control toggles between floating and docked. Docked, the panel becomes a full-height sidebar that pushes the grid left rather than overlaying it (see 1).

Surfaces that render outside the panel

Several elements live beyond the panel frame and persist as the analyst works:

  • the Ask Scout trigger and its first-run intro bubble
  • the minimized mini-bubble (Resume conversation)
  • grid cell highlighting and the per-row ready dots
  • the cross-page name tag and the Control-Panel alert cards
  • toasts
  • the bottom prototype-timer bar
One ring, always on An animated shimmer border rings the panel in both modes; it signals "a thinking machine" and is detailed under Motion.
Conversation lifecycle

Conversation lifecycle

Every exchange with Scout moves through three phases — ask, thinking, and answered — and each phase paints a distinct surface so the analyst always knows whether Scout is waiting, working, or done.

The three phases

A turn begins when the analyst clicks a suggestion chip, types a question in the composer, or runs a saved prompt or path. The empty conversation opens on the welcome surface — a greeting plus closed suggestion chips and a free-text composer.

Ask. The question appears immediately as a right-aligned user bubble carrying the analyst's exact wording.

Thinking. A left-aligned Scout bubble appears showing live thinking steps — for example Reading flagged rows → Clustering causes → Checking policy bands. Steps reveal progressively under a gentle shimmer. When reasoning completes, the steps auto-collapse into a Show details disclosure (see 1).

Answered. The bubble fills in top to bottom, in a fixed order described below.

Answer bubble anatomy

Reading down the answered bubble (see 1–7):

  • Scope tag — an All rows / This row indicator of what the answer covers.
  • Confidence line — a colored dot plus label, often with a short caveat such as Medium confidence · Cluster counts are estimated.
  • Answer body — one or more typed blocks (see below).
  • Action chips — scenario-specific next steps, e.g. Open 11 margin-guard rows, Open 8 over-band rows.
  • Details disclosure — collapsed reasoning and sources.
  • Answer footer — feedback controls and an overflow menu.
  • Follow-up chips — suggested next questions.

Answer-body block types

The body supports several block types, mixed as the answer requires:

  • Paragraph — free prose.
  • Bullet list — enumerated points.
  • Table — rows and columns.
  • Breakdown — a labeled tally, e.g. FLAGS BY CLUSTER — Margin guard 11, Above peer band 8, Stale benchmark 4, Total flagged 23.
  • CSV-link — an export affordance for the underlying data.

Confidence & sources

Confidence and sources are first-class, never decoration. The confidence line maps one of three levels to a color:

LevelDot color
HighGreen
MediumAmber
LowRed

The Show details · N steps · M sources disclosure expands to reveal the full thinking steps and the cited sources, each rendered as a clickable pill.

Feedback flow

The answer footer carries a thumbs-up / thumbs-down rating and a ⋯ overflow menu (copy, export, save, and similar).

Choosing thumbs-down opens an inline feedback form: a reason picker — wrong data / misread / made up — plus an optional note.

Submitting swaps the form for a receipt reading Thanks — this helps Scout improve.

Follow-ups

Under the heading TRY A FOLLOW-UP, Scout offers suggested next questions such as Where am I leaving margin?. Tapping one continues the conversation as a fresh ask.

Clearing & undo

The Clear control archives the current conversation and empties the surface; an Undo affordance restores it. Starting a new question after clearing retires the undo buffer, so the restore is no longer available.

Toast

At most one toast is shown at a time, anchored at the bottom of the viewport. It auto-dismisses and carries a success, warning, or error tone depending on the event.

Build note The phase order — ask, thinking, answered — and the fixed top-to-bottom order of the answered bubble are product rules. Every answer must carry an explicit confidence level and citable sources.
Threading & background work

Threading & background work

Scout is multi-threaded: every row — and the whole-view stream — can hold its own conversation that keeps working in the background while the analyst does other things. Each conversation moves through a small, well-defined state machine, and the moment any one of them finishes, a coordinated set of surfaces lights up together so the answer is impossible to miss.

Three thread states

A conversation is always in exactly one of three states. State drives every status affordance described below.

StateMeaningHow it reads
in-progressScout is still thinking on this conversationAnimated dots, a quiet shimmer
readyAn answer is waiting but has not been openedSolid dot plus an accent rail; counted as unseen
viewedThe answer has been opened and seenMuted; no longer counted

An unseen count aggregates every conversation currently in the ready state. Opening a ready conversation moves it to viewed and decrements that count.

Simulated think time & the single clock

In the prototype, a question takes a fixed ~20 seconds to move from in-progress to ready — a stand-in for real model latency. One global clock drives every surface, so countdowns and status flips stay in lockstep no matter which page the analyst is on.

Prototype-only The fixed 20-second delay and the shared clock are demo scaffolding. A production build replaces them with real streaming and live status; the lockstep behavior is what matters, not the number.

Surfaces that flip together on ready

When a thread crosses into ready, all of these update in the same beat:

  • The per-row dot for that row lights and begins pulsing.
  • The Chats unseen badge increments.
  • The cross-page name tag reads Scout is ready.
  • A card appears in the Control Panel for that thread.

Chats inbox

The Chats tab lists conversations as question-led cards (see 1, 2). Each card shows the lead question, a one-line answer preview, a timestamp (for example Jun 1 · 4:41 PM), a status badge (animated dots while thinking, a solid dot with an accent rail when ready, muted once viewed), a star toggle, and a ⋯ menu (favorite, delete, save). The tab itself carries the unseen count badge (see 3), and a control to start a fresh conversation sits at the top (see 4). Clicking a card slides into that conversation.

Per-row ready indicator

A pinned left-gutter marker of three dots signals each row's background state (see 1): lit and pulsing when that row's answer is ready, a quiet shimmer while in-progress, and absent when the row has been viewed or has no conversation at all. Clicking the marker jumps straight into that row's conversation.

Open question Whether the per-row dot should appear for every backgrounded row or only for rows the analyst explicitly asked about is an unresolved product decision.
Scoping & context

Scoping & context

Every conversation is anchored to a context — the slice of the grid the analyst is asking about. Context is captured as removable chips in a bar at the top of the Chats view, set by clicking cells in the grid, and mirrored back as colored highlighting on the grid itself. With nothing in focus, Scout answers about the whole view.

Context chips

The context bar holds one or more chips describing the current scope. Three kinds exist:

  • Row chip — a whole row (one customer / location / product line).
  • Cell chip — one specific value, such as a single proposed price.
  • Selection chip — a multi-cell pick made within the scoping rules.

When empty, the bar shows the hint Nothing in focus · click a cell alongside a + Add a cell affordance (see 1, 2). When populated, each chip is a removable pill.

Default click behavior

While the panel is open, clicking a grid cell replaces the current non-pinned context with a fresh chip — a cell chip for a data column, a row chip for an identity column. Once any chip is explicitly pinned, further clicks append instead of replace. Clicking a chip's own target toggles that chip off. With the panel open, Scout's scoping intercepts the grid's normal click so a click scopes Scout rather than opening the row's drawer.

Click interception Interception applies only while the panel is open. When Scout is closed, grid clicks behave normally. A rebuild must scope Scout without breaking the grid's own row interactions.

+Add mode and the no-diagonal rule

The + Add a cell affordance enters a focused picking mode: the cursor becomes a copy cursor and eligible cells gain a dashed outline. A multi-cell pick must share one row or one scope-group column — never a diagonal. Off-axis cells and identity cells are disabled while picking (see 1, 2). Pressing Esc exits the mode.

Grid cell highlighting

Highlighting appears only while Scout is open, and it encodes scope at a glance:

TreatmentMeaning
Soft green tint, inset outline, accented textScope cells — what the current context covers
Faint grey overlayIdentity cells — the row's descriptive columns
Subtle background tint per groupColumn-group tints — Cost, Proposed, Margin, Δ-vs-prior, Flags
Violet outline badgeAn explicit cell chip, marking its exact cell

The "How cell colors work" tour

A How cell colors work text link near the grid's toolbar opens a popover explaining identity versus scope coloring (see 1). It replaces an earlier color-key legend strip, which was retired in favor of this lighter affordance.

Library, Save & Share

Library, Save & Share

The Library is where reusable questions live, and where in-the-moment conversations get promoted into durable, shareable assets. It holds two kinds of saved item and gives the analyst a calm path from "I just asked this twice" to "this is now a one-click routine my team can run."

Two kinds of saved item

Every saved item is either a Prompt — a single reusable question — or a Path — an ordered list of questions that run in sequence. Both carry a name, a description, a scope (row-level versus aggregate/whole-view), a subject tag, a star for favoriting, and optional sharing.

Library cards, search & filters

The Library is list-first. A toolbar across the top offers a search field, a starred-only toggle, and an ownership filter with three states: All Yours Shared with you (see 1). Each card stacks a title with an actions row, then a meta row of pills — a kind pill reading Path · N steps or Prompt alongside scope and subject (see 2) — then a description clamped to two lines (see 5), then a footer carrying provenance on the left and the primary action on the right. Edit and Share reveal on hover; the star stays visible at all times (see 4).

Two empty states cover the surface: a "nothing saved in this tab" state when a tab has no items, and a "no matches" state when filters or search exclude everything, paired with a Clear filters affordance.

Running: Run vs Open

The card footer's action depends on the item kind (see 3). Run executes a path's steps sequentially — Scout switches to the chat view and streams each answer in turn. Open drops a prompt's text into a new ask, ready to send. A row-scoped item carries a guard: if it is run with no row in context, Scout surfaces a warning to Pick a row first rather than answering against the wrong scope.

The Save modal

Saving opens a two-tab modal. The Details tab collects a required name, an optional description, a subject, and the step list. Save stays disabled until there is both a name and at least one kept step.

The Share tab carries an audience picker built from scope cards — Only me Team Specific people — with a scrollable avatar list revealed when "Specific people" is chosen. The same picker control powers both this tab and the standalone Share modal, so the audience experience is identical in both places.

The inline path editor

Within the Details tab, a path's steps are directly editable: each step can be renamed inline, reordered up or down, or dropped. A dropped step renders struck-through and can be restored rather than vanishing. A Flatten on save option collapses the whole sequence into a single prompt for analysts who want one question instead of a multi-step run.

The path-candidate nudge

After the analyst runs two or more questions of the same scope in a row, Scout offers an inline prompt beneath the latest answer: Save these N questions on this row as a path? (or "on the quote book" for whole-view scope). It previews the step labels, offers a Save as path button, and a dismiss control.

Appears once per qualifying run. The nudge collapses after saving or dismissing, and questions already saved are excluded from later candidates so the same group is never re-suggested.

The standalone Share modal

Opened from a card's overflow menu, the Share modal headers with the item name (see 1), presents the shared audience picker (see 2), and confirms with a Share action (see 3). One difference from the Save modal's Share tab: the standalone modal omits the "Only me" option, since sharing nothing privately is not a meaningful action here.

Cross-page chrome

Cross-page chrome

Scout keeps working after the analyst leaves the Quote Book, so a thin layer of status surfaces lives outside the panel and persists across navigation. Each one reflects the same underlying activity state — idle, thinking, or ready — and each offers a path back into the conversation that produced it.

Trigger and activity badge

The Ask Scout toolbar control carries a small status badge that mirrors Scout's current activity: idle, thinking, or ready. The badge gives the analyst an at-a-glance read on whether background work is in flight or an answer is waiting, without opening the panel. The trigger also opens the panel via ⌘G / Ctrl+G.

Name tag

A compact tag sits near the user's name in the app header and reads Scout is thinking, Scout is ready, or an idle state, accompanied by an unseen dot when answers are waiting. It updates in lockstep with every other ready surface the moment a conversation finishes.

Control-Panel alert cards

Scout replaces the app's "Recent Alerts" empty state with a card for each ready conversation. Clicking a card (see 1) navigates back to the Quote Book and opens that exact conversation, so an analyst who wandered off can return to a finished answer in one move.

Minimized mini-bubble

Minimizing collapses the panel to a small violet pill anchored just above the trigger, labelled Resume conversation, with a gentle sheen. Clicking it reopens the full panel with all state intact — context, conversation, and position preserved.

First-run intro bubble

On first visit a one-time tip points at the trigger: Hi, I'm Scout 👋 — Your pricing co-pilot. Ask about any row — costs, margins, deltas. Click to start, or press ⌘G. It appears once and does not return on later visits.

Bottom prototype-timer bar

A deliberately utilitarian fixed strip spans the bottom of the viewport — dashed top border, monospace type, neutral greys with an amber accent — reading PROTOTYPE TIMER (simulated latency — not part of Scout) alongside a soonest-ready countdown (see 1, 2).

Demo-only — do not build. The timer bar exists solely to make the simulated think-time legible during demos. It is not part of the product. A real build wires conversations to live streaming and status; the simulated latency and this bar both disappear.
Motion & micro-interactions

Motion & micro-interactions

Motion is present but restrained — it signals a calm "thinking machine," never bounces or distracts. Every effect is slow, low-contrast, and purposeful; together they make the empty state feel alive, the work-in-progress feel legible, and view changes feel spatially grounded.

The signature effect is the shimmer border that rings the panel: a multi-color ring sweeping indigo into violet into magenta, with two glints orbiting the edge at different rates and directions so the panel reads as actively reasoning. At rest it stays a crisp 2px ring with no heavy glow. The same restraint governs the thinking state inside the body: live thinking steps and the inter-message thinking… divider play a gentle gradient sweep only while a turn is active, settling to static once the answer resolves.

Spatial transitions reinforce the panel's information architecture. The panel enters with a short upward translate plus fade. Switching between views animates as a horizontal push/pop whose direction matches the nav direction, so moving deeper and backing out feel like physical movement. Two small ambient touches add warmth: the welcome mascot drifts in a slow idle sway, and suggestion chips fire a one-shot diagonal gradient glimmer on hover.

Considerations & open questions

Considerations & open questions

Scout is specified as a working behavioral prototype, which means several seams between the demo and a production build are deliberate. The items below are the decisions a rebuild must own, followed by the behavioral questions still open from review.

Production considerations a rebuild must decide

Real model swap

In the prototype every answer, thinking step, source, and confidence rating is canned, and each question takes a fixed simulated delay (~20 seconds) driven by a single global demo clock so that every surface flips status in lockstep. A real build wires the ask path to a live model or service: the fixed latency and the global clock both disappear, replaced by genuine streaming output and real per-request status. The three-phase turn (ask → thinking → answered) and its surfaces stay; only what fills them becomes real.

Real data-grid integration

Cell highlighting, the scope-group tints, the per-row ready indicator, and click interception all assume tight coupling to the real grid's column model and event handling. The hard constraint here is click interception: while Scout is open, a click on a cell must scope Scout rather than triggering the grid's own row interaction (e.g. opening a row drawer), and that interception must be reversible the moment Scout closes — without permanently breaking native grid behavior.

Persistence, identity & team roster

Saved Prompts and Paths, sharing, stars, and conversation history are all in-memory in the prototype and vanish on reload. A real build needs durable storage, an authenticated user to own saved items, and a team roster to populate the share picker's Specific people avatar list.

Preserve the scope & append rules

Two interaction rules are product decisions, not prototype artifacts, and must survive a rebuild: the pick constraint (a multi-cell selection shares one row OR one scope-group column, never a diagonal) and the chip behavior (clicks replace the active context until a chip is explicitly pinned, after which clicks append).

Confidence & sources are first-class. Every answer must carry an explicit confidence rating and citable sources. This is core to Scout's trustworthiness, not visual decoration — an answer that cannot state how sure it is or where it looked should be treated as incomplete.

Always-on provider above navigation

Scout's background work and its status clock must persist as the analyst moves between pages. The state that owns threads and timing therefore has to live above the app's navigation layer; scoping it per-page resets in-flight background work every time the analyst navigates away.

Open behavioral questions surfaced in review

  • Whether the per-row ready indicator should appear for every backgrounded row, or only for rows the analyst explicitly asked about.
  • Whether the prototype timer bar should be hidden on authentication / login surfaces.
  • Whether running a Path should let the analyst choose scope at save time, or always inherit the scope of the source conversation.
  • Whether negative feedback should ever suppress or retract the answer, or only log the signal.
Not part of the product. The bottom prototype-timer bar and the simulated latency it counts down exist only to make the fake delay legible during demos. Neither ships — both are removed once real model latency replaces the simulation.
